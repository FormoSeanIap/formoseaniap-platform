After the infrastructure was stable and the engineering section was live, attention shifted to how the site actually looked. The cache collision from Part 6 was resolved, the CI/CD pipeline was deploying both sites cleanly, and the content was in place. The remaining work was visual — making the portfolio look like something a hiring manager would take seriously rather than something a developer threw together and never reviewed. A friend looking at the engineering landing page noticed the first problem: text broke before the container edge, leaving visible empty space on the right side of the hero section. The subtitle — "Platform Engineer with 4+ years of experience operating distributed..." — broke mid-sentence when there was clearly room for more words on the line.

That observation started a debugging iteration that went through five distinct stages over several days. Each stage narrowed the problem, even when earlier stages turned out to be wrong. The process mattered as much as the final CSS, because the instinct at each step was reasonable — and wrong in a way that only became clear after trying it.

## Stage 1: The Ragged Right Edge

The first instinct was the obvious one: add `text-align: justify` to fill each line edge-to-edge. Justified text eliminates the ragged right margin by stretching word spacing so that every line (except the last) reaches the container boundary. The hero subtitle would no longer break early with empty space on the right. Problem solved, or so it seemed.

I rejected justify after about ten minutes of research and testing. Three things killed it. First, unhyphenated English — which is what the site uses — creates visible "river" gaps between words when justified. Rivers are vertical or diagonal channels of whitespace that form when the browser stretches word spacing unevenly across consecutive lines. They are distracting and make the text look broken in a different way than the ragged edge did. Second, accessibility research consistently recommends left-aligned text for long-form reading. Justified text forces the eye to adjust to inconsistent word spacing on every line, which is measurably harder for readers with dyslexia or low vision. Third, the reference portfolio sites I had been studying — p5aholic.me, shoya-kajita.com, edwinle.com — all use left-aligned body text deliberately. These are designers who care about typography. If they chose left-aligned, there was probably a reason beyond habit.

What I added instead was `text-wrap: pretty` on paragraphs globally. This is a CSS property supported in Chrome 117+, Safari 17.4+, and Firefox 121+ that tells the browser to balance the last few lines of a paragraph and avoid orphan words — a single word sitting alone on the final line. It does not change the alignment or add justify-style word stretching. It just makes the line-breaking algorithm slightly smarter about where it breaks near the end of a block. The visual improvement was subtle but real: paragraphs no longer ended with a lonely two-letter word on the last line, and the overall texture of the text felt more intentional. No river gaps, no accessibility cost, no change to the fundamental left-aligned layout.

This was a good addition, but it did not fix the original problem. The hero subtitle still broke early. The ragged right edge was not caused by bad line-breaking — it was caused by something constraining the text width below the available container width. `text-wrap: pretty` made the paragraphs look better, but the constraint was still there.

## Stage 2: The Measure-Cap Misfire

The second attempt came from a typography rule I had read about: cap prose at a reading-comfortable 65–70 characters per line. The idea is that lines longer than about 75 characters force the eye to travel too far horizontally, making it easy to lose your place when returning to the left margin. The standard recommendation is a "measure" of 50–75 characters, with 65–70 being the sweet spot for body text.

I applied this as `max-width: 65ch` on hero copy, `max-width: 70ch` on article paragraphs, and `max-width: 70ch` on timeline card bullets. The `ch` unit in CSS is the width of the "0" character in the current font, so `70ch` approximates 70 characters per line at the element's font size.

The result was the opposite of what I intended. A screenshot from a reviewer showed the caps making things worse, not better. At the site's typical card width of around 1000px, the `70ch` cap translated to roughly 560px at the body font size. That left nearly half the card empty on the right — a 440px dead zone with no content. The hero section looked even more broken than before, because now the text was visibly constrained to a narrow column inside a wide container, with nothing filling the remaining space.

The lesson was that typographic measure caps are a rule for columns wider than the measure, not a universal "make prose look nice" rule. If your container is already narrower than 70 characters, the cap does nothing — the text was already within the comfortable range. If your container is wider than 70 characters, the cap creates dead space unless you have a multi-column layout or a sidebar to fill the remaining width. On a single-column card at 1000px, the cap just made the layout look half-finished.

I rolled back the caps from `.hero-copy > p`, `.article-content p`, `.timeline li.card > p`, and `.timeline li.card ul li`. The `text-wrap: pretty` from Stage 1 stayed because it cost nothing and still helped with orphan words. But the measure caps were gone.

## Stage 3: The `.lead` Cap Surprise

The third stage was more targeted. After removing the broad measure caps, I noticed that the `.lead` class still had a `max-width` of about 56 characters. My mental model of `.lead` was "a short one-line tagline" — a brief label that sits under the page title and does not need much horizontal space. A 56-character cap seemed reasonable for a tagline.

The mental model was wrong. Every `.lead` on the site was actually wrapping a two-to-three sentence subtitle, because every page template put the long summary paragraph inside `.lead`. The engineering landing page's subtitle — "Platform Engineer with 4+ years of experience operating distributed systems, building CI/CD pipelines, and managing cloud infrastructure on AWS" — was not a tagline. It was a full professional summary. The `.lead` class had drifted from its original intent (short label) to its actual usage (multi-sentence subtitle) without anyone updating the CSS constraint to match.

That was why the engineering landing subtitle broke at "distributed" and "engineering" with space on the right. The `.lead` cap was sitting at roughly 56 characters while the column had room for roughly 85 characters. The text was being forced to wrap early by a constraint that assumed it would never be longer than one short line.

The fix was to loosen `.lead` from 56ch to 85ch. At the site's current viewport widths, 85 characters fills the column naturally without creating dead space. On wider displays — 4K monitors, ultrawide screens — 85 characters still stays within reading comfort. The subtitle stopped breaking early, and the hero section finally used the available width.

The meta-lesson was about global constraints and class drift. "Cap once, globally" is only safe when you know every place the class is used and can verify that the constraint makes sense for all of them. When a class drifts from its original intent — when `.lead` stops being a tagline and starts being a subtitle — the global constraint becomes a global bug. Before tuning any CSS constraint on a utility class, spot-check every usage in the codebase. The five minutes of grepping saves the hours of debugging why one page looks broken while another looks fine.

## Stage 4: The Missing `data-page` Grid Rule

The fourth stage was a different kind of bug. The `.lead` fix had resolved the hero subtitle wrapping on the engineering landing page, but a comparison between `/engineer/projects.html` and `/projects.html` revealed a new discrepancy. On the same viewport width, the engineering projects page rendered its hero text across three lines — "Real projects, / shaped by real / constraints." — while the main site's projects page rendered the same-length hero as a single line. The HTML was virtually identical. The content was similar in length. The viewport was the same. But one page wrapped aggressively and the other did not.

The diagnosis started with the `.hero` section's grid layout. The hero defaults to `grid-template-columns: 1.6fr 1fr`, which is designed for pages that have a sidebar element next to the hero text — the landing page uses this to place a profile image or a call-to-action beside the introduction. The `1.6fr 1fr` split gives the text column about 62% of the hero width and the sidebar about 38%.

Pages that do not have a sidebar need to opt out of this two-column layout. The opt-out is done through `data-page` attribute selectors in the CSS. Each page sets a `data-page` attribute on the `<body>` element — `data-page="projects"`, `data-page="eng-home"`, `data-page="about"` — and the CSS includes rules like `body[data-page="projects"] .hero { grid-template-columns: 1fr; }` that override the default two-column grid with a single full-width column.

The CSS had opt-out rules for `projects`, `eng-home`, and `about`. It did not have rules for `eng-projects` or `eng-articles`. When the engineering section was created by cloning the main site's page patterns, the HTML templates were duplicated and the `data-page` attributes were updated to the new values — but nobody grepped the CSS for every `data-page` selector that the original pages depended on. The main site's `/projects.html` had a `data-page="projects"` opt-out that gave it a single-column hero. The engineering site's `/engineer/projects.html` had `data-page="eng-projects"`, which matched no CSS rule, so it fell through to the default `1.6fr 1fr` grid. The hero text was being squeezed into 62% of the available width, which is why it wrapped across three lines while the main site's version did not.

The fix was two lines of CSS: add `body[data-page="eng-projects"] .hero` and `body[data-page="eng-articles"] .hero` to the existing selector list with `grid-template-columns: 1fr`. The engineering projects and articles pages immediately matched the layout of their main-site counterparts.

The meta-lesson was about cloning page patterns across sections. When you duplicate a page template to a new section and give it a new `data-page` value, grep the entire CSS for every `data-page` selector that the original page depends on. Each selector is an opt-out from some default behavior, and missing even one means the new page silently inherits a default that was never intended for it. The symptom — a three-line hero versus a one-line hero — was obvious once you saw both pages side by side, but the cause was buried in a CSS selector list that nobody thought to update.

## Stage 5: The CSS Grid `min-content` Trap

The fifth stage was the hardest bug in the set, and the one that took the longest to diagnose. On one specific article — a DevOps piece that included a wide ASCII table showing `DescribeNetworkInterfaces` API output — paragraphs and headings rendered at roughly 50% of the card width. The text was not wrapping early because of a character cap or a grid column split. It was being constrained to about half the available space, with the other half empty. The ASCII table itself had horizontal scrolling, which was correct behavior for a wide `<pre>` block. But the paragraphs above and below the table — normal prose with no special formatting — were also narrow, as if something was squeezing the entire content column.

The investigation started with the obvious suspects. `.article-content` had no `max-width`. No `width: N%`. No explicit constraint of any kind. The card container had no unusual padding or margin that would eat half the width. The only differences between this article and ones that rendered correctly were the ASCII tables and several code blocks with lines longer than 140 characters — lines that could not be broken by the browser because they were inside `<pre>` elements where whitespace is preserved.

The root cause was in how CSS Grid handles implicit column sizing. The `.card` element was `display: grid` with no explicit `grid-template-columns` declaration. When a grid container has no explicit column template, CSS Grid creates implicit columns using `auto` sizing. The `auto` track sizing algorithm uses `min-content` as the track's minimum size. This means the column will never be narrower than the widest piece of unbreakable content inside it.

Here is where the `<pre>` block entered the picture. The `<pre>` element containing the ASCII table had a `min-content` width equal to its widest unbreakable line — the full width of the `DescribeNetworkInterfaces` output, which was well over 1000 pixels. The grid column's minimum size was set by this `min-content` value. The `<pre>` block also had `overflow-x: auto`, which meant it would show a horizontal scrollbar rather than expanding the visible area. But `overflow-x: auto` does not change the element's contribution to the grid track sizing algorithm. The grid still reserved space based on the `<pre>`'s intrinsic `min-content` width, even though the `<pre>` itself would scroll horizontally within whatever space it was given.

The net effect was that the grid column was sized based on the widest `<pre>` line, but the visible rendering was constrained by the card's actual width. The paragraphs — which are normal flow content and respect the column width — rendered within the constrained visible width of the column, which was narrower than the card because the grid's internal sizing math had allocated space differently than the visible layout suggested. The symptom was narrow paragraphs. The cause was a wide `<pre>` block in the same grid column, influencing the track sizing in a way that was invisible in the DOM inspector unless you knew exactly what to look for.

The fix was a single line: `grid-template-columns: minmax(0, 1fr)` on `.card`. The `minmax(0, 1fr)` pattern is the canonical CSS Grid fix for "let the column shrink below `min-content`." By setting the track minimum to `0` instead of the default `auto` (which resolves to `min-content`), the grid column is free to be as narrow as the container requires. The `1fr` maximum means the column fills all available space. The `<pre>` block's `overflow-x: auto` still provides horizontal scrolling for long lines, but the grid no longer reserves extra space based on the `<pre>`'s intrinsic width. Paragraphs fill the full card width. The ASCII table scrolls horizontally within the card. Everything renders as intended.

This bug was hard to spot for four reasons. Nothing in the DOM looked wrong — there was no visible CSS property constraining the paragraph width. Nothing in the applied styles showed an explicit width or `max-width` on any ancestor. The bug only surfaced on articles that happened to have wide `<pre>` content, so most articles rendered correctly and the problem seemed article-specific rather than structural. And the visible symptom — narrow paragraphs — was nowhere near the cause — wide `<pre>` blocks in the same grid column. The distance between symptom and cause made it nearly impossible to diagnose by visual inspection alone. It required understanding how CSS Grid's track sizing algorithm interacts with `min-content` and `overflow`, which is not something most developers carry in working memory.

The general rule: whenever you use `display: grid` on a container that holds user-generated or dynamic content — articles, comments, code blocks, tables, anything with unpredictable width — add `grid-template-columns: minmax(0, 1fr)` (or repeat the pattern for each track) to prevent wide inline content from hijacking the column sizing. This one line of CSS is the kind of fix that saves hours the next time you encounter it.

## Diacritics Rendering

Between the grid debugging stages, a smaller but visually jarring issue surfaced with the site's brand name. The name "Sêng-Gān Ia̍p" uses Pe̍h-ōe-jī romanization, which includes diacritics like macrons (the horizontal bar over ō), circumflexes (the hat over ê), and combining marks (the vertical line in a̍). These are not exotic Unicode — they are standard Latin characters with combining diacritical marks, used across dozens of romanization systems for East Asian languages.

The problem was the font stack. The CSS had `font-family: 'Noto Sans JP', ...` as the first entry, because the site includes Japanese and Chinese content and Noto Sans JP has excellent CJK coverage. But Noto Sans JP's Latin character support, while functional, has poor positioning for macrons and combining marks on Latin letters. The macron over "ō" sat too high, the combining mark in "a̍" was misaligned, and the overall rendering of the brand name looked broken — like a font rendering bug rather than intentional diacritics.

The fix was to reorder the font stack: move Manrope (the site's primary Latin font) to the first position, so Latin characters — including those with diacritics — render with Manrope's glyph metrics. CJK characters that Manrope does not cover fall through to Noto Sans JP as the second entry. The browser's font fallback mechanism handles this automatically: for each character, it tries the first font in the stack, and if that font does not have a glyph for the character, it moves to the next font. Latin characters with diacritics render with Manrope. CJK characters render with Noto Sans JP. Both look correct.

A related issue appeared on the landing page title, where the brand name was clipped at certain viewport widths. The title used a `clamp()` function for responsive font sizing, but the minimum value in the clamp was too large for narrow viewports when the diacritics added extra vertical space. Lowering the `clamp()` minimum and adding `max-width: 100%` to the title element resolved the clipping. The title now scales smoothly from mobile to desktop without cutting off the diacritics or overflowing the container.

## Debugging-Process Takeaways

Five stages, five different root causes, and only one of them — the `text-wrap: pretty` addition in Stage 1 — survived unchanged into the final CSS. Every other stage involved trying something reasonable, discovering it was wrong or incomplete, and using that failure to narrow the search. The process was not efficient in the traditional sense. It was iterative, and several of the iterations were dead ends. But each dead end eliminated a hypothesis and pointed toward the actual cause.

Layered fixes work when you treat each layer as a hypothesis rather than a solution. Stage 1 eliminated `text-align: justify` and added `text-wrap: pretty`. Stage 2 eliminated broad measure caps. Stage 3 found the `.lead` constraint. Stage 4 found the missing grid opt-outs. Stage 5 found the `min-content` trap. Each stage built on the previous one's failure, and the final result was a set of targeted fixes rather than a single sweeping change.

Read reference sites' CSS before inventing your own rules. The portfolio sites I studied — p5aholic.me, shoya-kajita.com, edwinle.com — all use left-aligned text, no measure caps on single-column layouts, and explicit grid track sizing. If I had inspected their CSS before reaching for `justify` and `max-width: 70ch`, I would have skipped two stages entirely. Reference sites are not just design inspiration — they are debugging shortcuts.

Do not reach for `text-align: justify` as the first fix for ragged text. Measure, column structure, and grid sizing explain 90% of apparent text alignment problems. The ragged right edge on this site was caused by a character cap on `.lead`, a missing grid opt-out, and a `min-content` track sizing issue — none of which had anything to do with text alignment. Justify would have masked the symptom while leaving the structural causes in place.

`minmax(0, 1fr)` on grid columns deserves a dedicated note in any personal knowledge base. It is the kind of one-line fix that is impossible to discover by visual inspection, easy to forget between encounters, and applicable to any grid container that holds dynamic content. The next time paragraphs look inexplicably narrow inside a grid, check whether a wide child element is inflating the track's `min-content` minimum.

A short glossary of the terms that mattered across these five stages, for reference:

**Measure** — the number of characters per line in a block of text. The typographic sweet spot is 50–75 characters. Relevant when deciding whether to apply `max-width` in `ch` units.

**min-content** — the smallest size a box can be without overflowing its content. For text, this is the width of the longest unbreakable word. For `<pre>` blocks, this is the width of the longest line. CSS Grid uses `min-content` as the default minimum for `auto`-sized tracks.

**Track sizing** — the algorithm CSS Grid uses to determine the width (or height) of each column (or row). Tracks can be sized with fixed values (`200px`), flexible values (`1fr`), or intrinsic values (`auto`, `min-content`, `max-content`). The `minmax()` function sets a range.

**text-wrap: pretty** — a CSS property that tells the browser to optimize line breaking for visual quality, avoiding orphan words and balancing the last few lines of a paragraph. Unlike `text-align: justify`, it does not change word spacing.

**text-wrap: balance** — a related property that distributes lines as evenly as possible across the entire block, not just the last few lines. More aggressive than `pretty`, and best suited for short blocks like headings rather than long paragraphs.

**text-align: justify and river gaps** — justified text stretches word spacing so every line reaches both margins. In unhyphenated text, this creates "rivers" — visible vertical channels of whitespace that form when stretched gaps align across consecutive lines. Rivers are a readability problem, not just an aesthetic one.
