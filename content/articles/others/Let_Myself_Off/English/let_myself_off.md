It was probably around the time I was admitted to a graduate program in philosophy that I felt most high-spirited in my life.

As a student from an unrelated field, I spent a year preparing, ended up ranking first in both NTU and NCCU, and even wrote a long, enthusiastic [reflection piece](../../Graduate_School_Philosophy_Exams/Mandarin/Exam_Strategy_Guide/zh.md).

Reading it again now, the confidence seeps through every line. I never expected that, in just about six years, I would find myself on a completely different path.

Today, I work as an engineer at a tech giant. On the surface, my current situation still seems describable as “successful.”

I managed to change careers within half a year, my income reached levels I once could hardly imagine, I published a book by sheer coincidence, and simply mentioning the company name I work for is often enough to catch people’s attention.

Yet it was precisely at this point that I began to feel a sense of confusion stronger than ever before.

I no longer knew what I was really doing, nor was I certain about what I truly wanted.

My daily rhythm became repetitive: working Monday through Friday, returning home each night exhausted; weekends rarely left me with enough energy to do what I actually wanted to do. It was only on Sunday nights that anxiety would slowly surface—because another week, another cycle, was about to begin.

## Inspiration from Recording a Podcast

A few months ago, I was invited to record [a podcast episode](https://open.spotify.com/episode/4UegijiJrVlAEhT3kFijXI?si=p2zGbsX7S2ea1dIOit67Ug) because of [a series of essays I had written years ago on *Attack on Titan*](../../../reviews/Anime_Manga/Attack_on_Titan/Humanity_Part1_Philosophy/English/humanity_part1_philosophy.md).

Through that recording, I met a friend who had chosen to leave the conventional work system and decided to make a living through creative work.

I found myself genuinely admiring her. Part of me seemed to be shouting from within, reminding me that the dreams I had temporarily shelved—or even deliberately ignored—for the sake of work were still there.

What exactly am I doing? What is the point of earning more money? I fix this bug for a client—then what? What kind of life do I actually want?

At the same time, I noticed a stronger sense of comparison growing within me than ever before.

Watching what my peers were doing, it felt as if I wanted to spur myself on through others’ success—yet, in a darker corner of my mind, I also hoped to find comfort in others’ failures.

## The Labels of Success and the Struggle of Failure

A few days ago, I met a senior who had just returned to Taiwan for a meal. He is now a PhD candidate.

Over the years, he may well have been the comparison figure I had always imagined. If I had chosen to continue in academia back then, would I really have had a chance to reach his level?

I now work as an engineer, producing results that ultimately belong to a company and its clients. Every paper he writes, however, is truly his own thought.

Did I leave academia purely for financial reasons? Or was it that I simply didn’t dare to admit that I might not have had the ability to continue down that path?

What struck me most was that he told me he thought I was actually a very successful person—while he had never once considered himself to be a “winner in life.”

He spoke of the frustration and pain of pursuing a PhD, saying that these feelings had already begun during his master’s years; all along, he had felt that he was falling behind his peers.

In truth, we had both seen each other’s achievements.

Once we attached those achievements to one another like labels, we saw success—but overlooked the failures and struggles hidden behind them.

There is no denying that labels make it convenient to understand others.

In reality, everyone begins by striving for those labels.

Only after obtaining them did I realize how much failure lies behind them, unseen. And it was then that I began to wish to shed those labels—to be seen for who I truly am.

Even if that self is ugly and chaotic.

## Reassessing My Current State

Recently, I reread [*Three Days of Happiness*](../../../reviews/Anime_Manga/Three_Days_of_Happiness/en.md), a work about how the value of life is measured.

From society’s objective standards, to the perspectives of significant others, and finally—to what matters most—self-evaluation.

I then revisited [*Train Dreams*](../../../reviews/Movie/Train_Dreams/en.md), a story of being haunted by the ghosts of the past, quietly spending a lifetime in boundless solitude.

Looking at myself now, perhaps I am a composite of these two works.

I am still trapped by my past self, still trying to chase the labels of success endorsed by society.

On the surface, I seem to be moving forward, but in reality, my gaze is constantly turned back toward certain moments in the past.

And so, every path I choose feels as though it is chosen only because it bears the shadow of the past—because it offers a sense of reassurance.

But perhaps, at this very moment, it is time to try once again to understand what I truly want.

To try to knock open a small crack in the cage of the past, and to step onto an unknown path—even if I have no idea what kind of scenery lies ahead.

## Conclusion

In any case, looking back, I really have done quite a lot over these past years.

To be able to feel these confusions and inner tensions as I approach thirty, perhaps I am still, in my own way, a fortunate person.
