*Train Dreams* tells the story of a logger in early 20th-century America, a man as ordinary as one could be.

The work portrays nothing more than a simple life: a simple man, a simple history, a seemingly simple era.

Yet perhaps that era wasn’t so simple after all. It was the age of rapid railroad expansion, a time when the old world slowly dissolved and the new one surged forward at full speed.

And this ordinary man, in the end, received a cruel joke played on him by the era itself.

## Time, Memory, and Life

The work feels slow and heavy, as if pulling the viewer quietly into the depths of that desolate time.

Loneliness, helplessness, and silence seep through the cracks of the narrative.

To me, it feels as though the protagonist’s life ended in that great fire. Everything after that burned-down moment was like the drifting of a ghost, floating endlessly through the ruins of memory.

He appears to move forward, yet his gaze never leaves the past.

Indeed, we are all made of fragments of our past.

We shape our present selves through memory, and yet we are also trapped by it, haunted, tugged at by unfinished emotions and words that were never spoken.

It is only in his conversation with Claire Thompson, the government worker sent to prevent another devastating fire, and later, in the final biplane ride, that time seems to flow again, even if just a little.

But in the end, it is still only a dream.

## Infinite Loneliness

Perhaps at different stages of life, we see different shades in *Train Dreams*.

It feels like this: the road so far has always been this way, and the road ahead will likely be the same.

I once believed that someday something different would appear, a revelation, a calling, something that would lead me toward a new direction.

But in truth, we are merely looking back at our past.

What is the meaning of life? When I search for it with all my strength, it remains elusive. And yet, in fleeting moments, it feels as if it’s right there in front of me.

Perhaps it has always been scattered throughout the small details of everyday life, hidden in each seemingly insignificant experience.

For some reason, as I watched this work, one phrase kept surfacing in my mind: infinite loneliness.

Even in the scenes where the protagonist drives his wagon and interacts with many different people, the loneliness seems to peak precisely there.

And when each person reaches the end of their life, perhaps this is the loneliness we must all eventually face.

I came into this world alone, and I will one day leave it alone.
