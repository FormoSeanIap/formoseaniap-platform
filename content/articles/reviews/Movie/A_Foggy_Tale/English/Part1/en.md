> I must study Politicks and War that my sons may have liberty to study Mathematicks and Philosophy. My sons ought to study Mathematicks and Philosophy, Geography, natural History, Naval Architecture, navigation, Commerce and Agriculture, in order to give their Children a right to study Painting, Poetry, Musick, Architecture, Statuary, Tapestry and Porcelaine.

> — John Adams

That was an age shrouded in fog — and it remains so, whether for those who lived inside it, or for us who look back from the present.

To be honest, the film hurt to watch from beginning to end. What hurt most was this: things I had once read — things that had existed only as words — were now laid out in front of me, vividly, through images.

The text alone is enough to suffocate; the images make it impossible to breathe.

### This is real history

Let’s turn the clock back, back into that era of dense mist.

At first, what surprised me most — though perhaps it shouldn’t have — was realizing how many people assumed large parts of the story were fictional.

Things as grotesquely absurd as a loved one being executed, and the family still having to pay to retrieve the body — yet many thought these were inventions of the screenwriter.

Fortunately — and unfortunately — some family members of political victims are still alive. Through oral history, we can still glimpse what was real. For example, see [the report for Watchout](https://watchout.tw/reports/vbCoK8CqGi6n8abgqAH0)[1]:

> *“As the number of executions surged, the existing execution grounds — ‘Machangting Execution Ground’ and ‘Ankeng Execution Ground’ — could no longer meet demand. The authoritarian government therefore moved political prisoners to what is now National Taiwan University’s Shuiyuan Campus, the ‘Shuiyuan Execution Ground.’ Because executions at Shuiyuan took place slightly later than those at Machangting — often during class hours — many students mention in their memoirs hearing gunshots at school or witnessing executions.”*

> *“The many stories of how families struggled to recover their relatives’ bodies show that after an execution, another harrowing road still awaited the victims. According to the Summary Report of the Survey on Historical Sites Related to Taiwan’s White Terror, in the early years those executed were handled by the ‘Jile Funeral Home’ — located where the Chang’an Pumping Station and part of Linsen Park are today — before families could retrieve the bodies or they were buried directly in the Liuzhangli cemetery area. But retrieving a body was never simple: families had to pay a substantial ransom. Some could not raise the money, did not dare to claim the body, or never even learned that their family member had been executed — especially in cases where mainlander victims were alone in Taiwan. Their bodies went unclaimed. As unclaimed corpses, some were buried in Liuzhangli Cemetery; some were sent to the National Defense Medical Academy for anatomy teaching and then buried in Jile Cemetery or cremated. With no one to offer rites for decades, the Liuzhangli burial area became a field of anonymous graves.”*

Although as I wrote this, NTU’s Department of Philosophy has already moved into the College of Liberal Arts newly built by the university, back when I was still a student, every time I stepped into the Philosophy building on Shuiyuan Campus, I felt a kind of damp chill and sticky clamminess — as if, even in the height of summer, air conditioning wasn’t necessary.

We can also consult another piece of [oral history iformation](https://www.civilmedia.tw/archives/22105) [2]. Through the letters that families received — some of which did not truly arrive until decades later — we can see the era’s reality at closer range:

> *“Lian! How deeply I have loved you… you know this. To have shattered your youth and be unable to repay you, and to leave this world first… alas! I have wronged you far too much! Compared with the depth of my love, I feel ashamed… Lian! At this moment, I earnestly hope you will remarry well. I hope you will seize a good match and a good opportunity, and bravely remarry. If, unfortunately, you do not meet a good match or a good opportunity, or if circumstances prevent you from remarrying, then you still need not live a hard-hearted, lonely, gray life. I sincerely pray that you live a happy, joyful life. In short, you must move forward on the path you yourself believe will be the happiest.”*

What kind of state of mind does it take to write a last letter to someone you love — asking them to remarry?

There are countless similar situations [3] [4]. And they are not legends, nor dramatized imagination; they are stories that truly happened.

With that recognition, perhaps it’s not hard to imagine why the doctor’s wife — who was “noticed” by the secret police — might appear to have lived with the agent.

Even if, deep down, she knew that this person could very well be the murderer of her husband, what choices did she have if she had to keep living with a child?

We might even say this: in that era, every Taiwanese person had, somewhere, at least one “body that might be waiting to be claimed.”

Yet more often than not, people waited and never saw their loved ones return — never saw a body they could retrieve.

They waited their whole lives, and still never received justice that belonged to them; they ultimately left this world carrying regret.

### Justice guarded by the times: guilt and forgiveness

If we’re going to talk about “justice,” we have to talk about the character who runs through the entire film — Zhao Gongdao.

If the protagonist, Ayue, embodies a kind of stubbornness and courage, then Zhao Gongdao is sheer vitality: crude, and yet irresistibly lovable.

One of the film’s most charged lines comes when he enters the mortuary, right after Ayue asks whether her brother’s body is there — and he blurts out, out of nowhere:

“Then is Liu Daqing’s body here?”

As the story unfolds, we gradually understand: the guilt he carries for a lifetime comes from the fact that, back then, he betrayed his friend Liu Daqing in order to survive, which led to Liu’s execution.

One simple line, and it threads through the burden of his entire life — precise enough to break your heart.

Another line, just as powerful, is delivered in a grotesquely absurd way. When Ayue demands that Zhao Gongdao “sell me,” and then take her brother’s remains back to Chiayi, Zhao replies:

“I’ve already carried three — how am I supposed to carry a fourth?”

Tragedy often arrives wearing the mask of comedy; this line is exactly that.

And the dynamic between the two is deeply endearing. There is a distinctly Taiwanese plainness and honesty there — tiny light flickering inside the fog, keeping each other alive.

You don’t have to understand politics to understand people: to understand the feeling of still choosing to support one another when life is hard; to understand the kindness of saving half a sesame bun for you; to understand the sense of duty that says, “If you get in my vehicle, I’ll take you to where you need to go.”

If you understand those things, then you probably also understand this: the kind of guilt where I cheated you out of a watch — and then spent my whole life unable to let it go.

That watch became Zhao Gongdao’s lifelong burden. In the end, he could only find forgiveness by returning it.

And when you see people like this, how could you not love this land — beautiful and sorrowful — and its people, lovable and resilient?
