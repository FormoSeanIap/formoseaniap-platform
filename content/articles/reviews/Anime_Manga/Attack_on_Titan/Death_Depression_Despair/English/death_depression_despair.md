"Death" plays a very important role in *Attack on Titan*. Like most shonen manga, the protagonists often fight their enemies while risking their lives, yet no matter how strong those enemies are, we usually do not expect the main cast to face a real chance of dying simply because they are not strong enough. In AoT, however, important characters die so quickly that it becomes reminiscent of *A Song of Ice and Fire* (*Game of Thrones*). In *A Song of Ice and Fire*, (**Spoiler**→) once readers begin to treat someone as an important character, that person may suddenly die in the next chapter, and the point of view then shifts to someone who had previously looked like a villain. (←**Spoiler**) AoT is not quite that extreme, since we can still point to Eren, Mikasa, and Armin as the three main characters, but beyond them the rest is much harder to say.

### Death Scenes

Death is everywhere in AoT, yet seeing a beloved character die is still a severe blow. In this section, I want to go over several of the death scenes that I consider most important, as far as my memory reaches. If you already know the story very well, you can skip this part.

The first is the death of Eren’s mother in *That Day* (その日), Ch. 2. That scene is extremely memorable, and it already hints at the darkness, gore, and depression of the entire work. The moment that truly shocked me, however, was in *First Battle* (初陣), Ch. 4, when Eren’s comrades die and Eren himself is swallowed alive. Of course, we later learn that Eren did not truly die, but that was exactly the point where I began to think this series was interesting and decided to keep following it. It also redefined what “risking one’s life” means in a shonen manga. Another unforgettable moment is in *A Dim Light Amid Despair* (絶望の中で鈍く光る), Ch. 5, when the woman of the couple Eren calls the “idiot pair” tries to perform CPR on the man whose body has been torn in half.

During the Battle of Trost District, Eren loses control after going berserk, and the elite Garrison soldiers assigned to protect him begin arguing over whether the mission should be abandoned. Their leader, Ian Dietrich, overrules everyone and insists that Eren must be protected first even though the decision will obviously lead to massive casualties, and he ultimately sacrifices his own life for it. In *Primitive Desire* (原初の欲求), Ch. 14, we are told that everyone is free from the moment they are born, and therefore no one needs a reason to fight for freedom. Yet another description in the same arc suggests that even if we reach the goal in the end, the price may be so high that it becomes impossible to feel happy about it.

![Chapter 14](../Shared/Ch14.png "Chapter 14")

The next group of scenes appears in the Female Titan arc, which includes the deaths of large numbers of ordinary soldiers as well as Levi’s Special Operations Squad. Unlike the Battle of Trost, the plan to capture the Female Titan fails completely, and several important characters, including Petra, die for nothing. The anime adaptation even adds the scene in which Petra’s corpse is thrown away in front of Levi. It is brutal.

As Armin argues in *Erwin Smith* (エルヴィン・スミス), Ch. 27, some people are always able, when facing a major decision, to abandon what must be abandoned more decisively than others. But this does not mean that sacrifice will necessarily bring the expected result. Sometimes one loses everything and gains nothing, just as happened in the Female Titan battle. That sequence was also the point when I realized that even important supporting characters could die at any moment under Isayama’s pen.

![Chapter 30](../Shared/Ch30.png "Chapter 30")

After that, I strangely felt relieved, because from that point on I could mentally prepare myself for any despair the story wanted to throw at me. At Utgard Castle, we watch the Scouts toyed with by the Beast Titan. Later, during the rescue of Eren, a huge number of soldiers die, Erwin loses his right arm, and Hannes is ironically eaten by the very titan he once failed to face. In the end, the survivors only make it back because Eren accidentally activates the Founding Titan’s power.

The battle to retake Wall Maria after the uprising arc is one of the most devastating battles in the whole work. Erwin also dies there, and only nine members of the Corps return alive. Yet it is precisely in this arc that Erwin’s speech before his death lifts the meaning of death and hope in AoT to another level.

![Chapter 80](../Shared/Ch80.png "Chapter 80")

After AoT enters the Marley arc, it reaches another height, and every death starts to feel both understated and unbearably heavy. The Survey Corps’ surprise assault on Marley produces something close to a massacre and indirectly causes Sasha’s death. Almost everyone on the island outside the Survey Corps is turned into a titan and killed because they drank Zeke’s spinal fluid. The Rumbling destroys nearly the whole world. Hange, Floch, and other side characters die one after another in the struggle against Eren. And in the then-latest chapter, *A Long Dream* (長い夢), Ch. 138, even Jean, Connie, and Gabi are turned into titans. Annie’s father, Reiner’s mother, and Falco’s parents are also turned just as they are about to reunite. It only made my stomach hurt again. I had thought I would no longer be heartbroken by any plot development.

![Chapter 138](../Shared/Ch138.png "Chapter 138")

### Death, Pain, Depression, and Despair

I think AoT has a distinctive talent for depicting depression and despair. The emotions that arise in the midst of its bloody, dark, and hopeless plot are hard to forget. From beginning to end, the story keeps telling us how cruel this world is, and the most striking part of that cruelty is the way it portrays death. In this section, I want to approach Isayama’s scenes of death from several angles.

### Sudden Death

If I talk about death in AoT, the first thing that comes to mind is sudden death. Any character may suddenly be handed a death sentence in the very next chapter, and that inevitably brings *A Song of Ice and Fire* to mind.

Examples of this kind of death include Eren’s squad in their first battle against titans, Levi’s Special Operations Squad facing the Female Titan, Sasha’s death, and the scene in which Jean, Connie, and Gabi are turned into titans, though at that point we still did not know whether they would actually die.

What all of these scenes share is that readers generally do not expect the character to die right away. Usually, they are preceded by warm or uplifting moments, and the contrast is exactly what makes them so painful. Eren’s first battle begins right after the team members talk hopefully about the future, only for almost all of them to be eaten immediately. Before Levi’s Special Operations Squad dies, their bond with Eren has just deepened because he decides to trust them, and Eld even uses the moment to bring up Petra and Oluo’s embarrassing stories. Sasha is then shot right after Connie expresses his feelings toward Jean and Sasha. Jean and Connie’s transformation into titans happens at the very moment many characters are on the verge of reunion.

Isayama is an author who knows very well how to torment his readers. He gives them a little hope first, then immediately pushes them into the abyss of despair. This kind of sudden death also confirms what Erwin says in his final speech: no matter what sort of life we live, death is fair to everyone. And perhaps that very fairness is what makes death so cruel.

![Chapter 4](../Shared/Ch04.png "Chapter 4")

### Despairing Death

If sudden death shows us that people can die easily, despairing death shows us a fate in which death cannot be avoided. I think scenes with this element include the deaths of the elite Garrison soldiers in Trost, Miche’s squad facing the Beast Titan at Utgard Castle, Erwin’s suicidal charge, and the deaths of Petra and Hannes.

What these scenes share is despair or endless battle, as if someone were secretly laughing at the miserable struggle of their lives from behind the curtain. Ian argues with Rico that the best humanity can do is nothing more than struggle with all its strength. Miche’s squad members are all elites, but no one can fight an endless war forever. Gelgar even finds a bottle before his death and becomes excited, only to discover in the end that it is empty. This kind of scene, as if someone were playing a tasteless joke on the characters, is pushed to the extreme when Levi learns of Petra’s feelings from her father. Hannes, meanwhile, finally finds a chance to overcome the shame of failing to protect Eren’s mother, only to be eaten in front of Eren and Mikasa by the same titan.

That feeling of despair is also portrayed brilliantly in Erwin’s suicidal charge. Floch has already broken down by then, arguing with Marlo that there is no need to protect the horses because no living person will be able to ride them anyway. As he says, nearly everyone present is about to die for reasons that make no sense to them, and all he can think of is endless retreat. That is why, when he later meets Hitch, he says that Marlo probably died with nothing but regret. It also makes me think of the very beginning, when almost Eren’s entire squad dies in their first battle against titans. Did they regret it? I think probably yes. Once you realize the price of freedom is that high, anyone would hesitate to move forward.

Isayama not only knows how to torment his readers; he is also extremely good at tormenting the characters he created. I explain this in much more detail when discussing Reiner in [From Reiner to Lannister, from Suicide to Freedom](../../Reiner_From_Suicide_To_Freedom/English/reiner_from_suicide_to_freedom.md). Characters in AoT are constantly forced to fight for survival in a battle with no visible end, where even a slight mistake means death. Readers can feel that despair very easily, and even when the characters somehow win a small victory, the price is so high that it is still hard to feel genuine joy.

### Random Death

Although characters in AoT die easily, we still have to admit that some people clearly just do not die. Isayama spends a great deal of effort making that feel reasonable, and one of the ways he does it is by repeatedly emphasizing the randomness of death.

The first time this appears is in the Battle of Trost. Ian is obviously the more valuable soldier, yet Rico, the one who argues against him, is the one who survives. During Erwin’s suicidal charge, the ones who die are Erwin and Marlo, not Floch. And in Chapter 138, even as the story approaches its end, the people turned into titans are simply the ones who happen not to possess titan powers. Their past character development has almost nothing to do with it.

As Erwin says in his final speech, no matter how wonderful or valuable our lives may be, death still arrives randomly. No one knows whether tomorrow will come first, or death will. Most of the people who are still alive are so only because of luck. Armin, for example, escapes death three times through exceptionally rare circumstances. The first time, Eren saves him. The second time, he is not crushed by the Female Titan because he knows Annie. The third time, he survives only because the commander’s life is sacrificed in an exceptionally cruel situation. (As for why Levi chooses Armin, see [Why Levi Does Not Save Erwin](../../Why_Levi_Does_Not_Save_Erwin/English/why_levi_does_not_save_erwin.md).) Random death is one of Isayama’s ways of telling readers how cruel the world is, and the fact that even those who try their hardest to live cannot escape it makes it especially painful.

### The Death of Others

No matter how tragic a person’s death may be, death only happens once for each individual. Experiencing the deaths of others, however, can happen many times, and that fear can accumulate without limit. As Daz says to Marco in *Response* (応える), Ch. 11, when his comrades die in front of him, the only thought he has is that at least it was not him.

Daz eventually collapses under the fear of not knowing when it will be his turn, because the only reason he is still alive is luck. His emotion is understandable, and it is depicted very vividly, but I do not think that is the real point here. What AoT portrays so brilliantly about the deaths of others is not merely the realization that you yourself might die as well, but the fact that a living individual is slipping away from you.

Death is not merely the passing of an individual; it is the ending of a story. Everyone has a unique story of their own, and that kind of thing cannot be replaced. What death means is the fact that this unique existence will never return.

AoT spends a great deal of time depicting this. In episode 11 of the anime, before the plan to plug the wall is carried out, Rico mentions many names to Eren and emphasizes that each name represents a complete story, that behind every name stands a real human being of flesh and blood. In *Dreams and Curses* (夢と呪い), Ch. 65, Eren cries and begs Historia to eat him because he can no longer bear the people who died because of him, and he names them one by one. Later, even after seeing Historia become queen, Eren still carries the same feeling and silently curses his own powerlessness while thinking of the dead in *Ruler of the Walls* (壁の王), Ch. 68.

![Chapter 65](../Shared/Ch65.png "Chapter 65")

Another excellent example is Levi, who deeply cares for his subordinates. In *Mercy* (慈悲), Ch. 32, Eren notices that Levi suddenly starts talking much more after his entire Special Operations Squad is wiped out, and realizes that it comes from Levi’s regret and loneliness. In *Counterfeit* (偽り者), Ch. 110, after hearing Zeke’s explanation, Levi insists that the destroyed village is Ragako Village, not just “that village.” What Levi is doing is reminding Zeke that what he destroyed was not some arbitrary place but Ragako Village, a place inhabited by individuals with different lives and stories. Finally, in *Savagery* (暴悪), Ch. 113, Levi even worries about where within the titan bodies his former subordinates might still be while he is cutting them down.

Isayama is extremely good at depicting the feelings of those left behind when a character dies, and by doing so he tells readers that what died was not merely a character but a living individual of flesh and blood. Yet that very method also makes the work sadder and more depressing. This is also why Eren gives up on himself during the uprising arc. He finally realizes that he only obtained titan power by accident, not because he was truly more special than others. Every soldier who died for him had a uniqueness and irreplaceability of their own, and without titan power some of them might even have been more valuable than Eren. Eren is simply the one who happened to survive.

### Meaningless Death

In *To You, 2,000 Years From Now* (二千年後の君へ), Ch. 1, the miserable Survey Corps encounter a mother who is searching for her son, and in the end all they can return to her is an arm they barely managed to recover. She asks the commander whether her son at least contributed anything to humanity’s future, but he can only cry and admit that this expedition gained nothing again.

This scene is vivid, and it also tightly grasps one of the central themes of the entire work. Of course it is true that nobody wants to die, but in reality we are also always searching for the meaning of death. If death is meaningful, or if it can obtain something, then death itself becomes less terrifying. That is why so many people sacrifice their lives for ideals such as freedom. In fact, AoT continually asks about the value of freedom and how much we are willing to sacrifice in exchange for it.

What makes AoT heavy and depressing is not death alone. What hurts even more is meaningless death. Our comrades are gone, and we gained nothing. In *What Should I Do Now?* (今、何をすべきか), Ch. 18, it is precisely this desire that death should at least bring something back that changes Jean. In the Female Titan arc, huge numbers of soldiers and Levi’s subordinates die, yet nothing is gained, and in the anime Petra’s body cannot even be brought home. What hurts most is that all those sacrifices buy absolutely nothing. Another example is when Floch argues with the protagonists and insists that even a nameless nobody like him should have the right to know what he is dying for.

On the other hand, even a mission that does achieve something can still be unbearable if the price is too high. We have already discussed this: both the Battle of Trost and the battle to retake Wall Maria are like this. That may also be why it is so hard to find any real joy in this work.

### The Meaning of Death

Of course, there are people in this world willing to sacrifice their lives for noble ideals, and they deserve our deepest respect. But that does not mean sacrifice will necessarily produce a good result. In fact, almost no one can know at the moment of sacrifice what exactly it will gain. The only certainty is that without sacrifice, nothing can be obtained at all. Therefore, the only thing we can do is try and wait for the result. In most cases, we cannot even know what role we will play in history.

This idea appears for the first time when Ian brings it up during the argument over whether to abandon Eren in Trost, but the most unforgettable version of it is Erwin’s speech before the suicidal charge. That speech raises the idea to another level. Before the speech, Floch asks Erwin whether everything is meaningless if life always ends at the same point. Erwin then responds with that classic claim: the meaning of life is not defined only by the person who lives it, but by those who follow after you. Therefore, everyone should do everything they can to honor the fallen and entrust the meaning of their lives to those who come later.

That speech is one of the most classic moments in AoT, though of course it does not let Erwin live. (If you are interested in Erwin, see [Erwin Smith Could Have Become the Real Demon-the Berserk in Attack on Titan](../../Erwin_The_Real_Demon/English/erwin_the_real_demon.md).) Still, it gives us a deeper way to feel the meaning of death. Human knowledge, too, is handed down from generation to generation like a long river, and each individual only occupies a small part of it.

Erwin’s speech also opens a different level of discussion about the meaning of death. From this perspective, a person’s life no longer belongs only to that person, but also to the comrades who have already died. If you want to die, then you must die for others rather than for yourself. This description resonates with what Eren says to Falco in *From One Hand to Another* (手から手へ), Ch. 97: everyone is pushed by something to step into hell, and most of the time those reasons are not really their own choice but the result of pressure from other people or the environment. In other words, we are still searching for the meaning of death, but the definition shifts from ourselves to others. (For more on Eren’s words here, see [Eren’s Hell, Justifying His Genocide](../../Eren_Hell_And_Genocide/English/eren_hell_and_genocide.md).)

Although Erwin is the one who says that the meaning of death is defined by those who follow, I think the best illustration of this is Sasha’s death. (See [Sasha and the Only Innocence](../../Sasha_And_Innocence/English/sasha_and_innocence.md).) Who defines the meaning of Sasha’s death? Clearly, it is Gabi. It is Sasha, in the end, who keeps Gabi from walking the same road as Reiner, and because of that Gabi can finally recognize the truth and abandon hatred. (See [Saving Gabi From the Dark Fate- What Nation Do You Belong to?](../../Saving_Gabi/English/saving_gabi.md).)

Another example is Marco’s death, whose meaning is defined by Jean. In *Night of the End* (終末の夜), Ch. 127, Hange reminds Jean of the comrades who already devoted their hearts, and the scene resembles what Erwin once said to Levi. We know that Marco’s death is exactly what made Jean become the Jean we now know, so the one who defines the meaning of Marco’s death is Jean. (See [What Makes Jean Angry?- The Burden of History](../../Jean_And_The_Burden_Of_History/English/jean_and_the_burden_of_history.md).)

On the other hand, Zeke offers another way of approaching the meaning of death. Because life is unavoidably full of despair, he thinks it would have been best if Eldians had never been born into this world at all. If one had never been born from the start, then of course there would be no possibility of a tragic death. Is that a satisfying answer? For Eren, clearly not. During the Battle of Trost, Armin once asks Eren why he still wants to go outside even after learning that only a hellish world awaits there, and Eren replies, “Because I was born into this world.”

This is Eren’s philosophy of life. He was once defeated and wanted to find a possibility of redemption in simply dying, but his mother’s words remind him of the importance of existence itself. In *Bystander* (傍観者), Ch. 71, she says, “You are already special because you were born into this world.” I think most readers, like me, have been saved by this line to some degree. Eren probably was too. Perhaps that is why he finally chooses to destroy the world rather than accept Zeke’s plan.

Every discussion presupposes existence, and so nothing takes priority over existence. To discuss the meaning of death is no different from discussing the meaning of life, because if a person can die, that means they once lived.
