With the release of Season 3, Episode 9 of the anime, Higuruma Hiromi's story has finally been fully laid out.

He might be my favorite character in Jujutsu Kaisen. Even compared with characters from other works, I still think his entrance is one of the most memorable and brilliantly executed sequences I have seen.

It was already impressive in the manga, but the quality of the anime adaptation unquestionably elevates this sequence by several levels, making me appreciate this character even more.

When I think about why I love this part so much, it is probably because of how skillfully it is written: it pushes the plot forward while also conveying ideas that genuinely move people.

# A Brilliantly Structured Sequence

When I say the writing is skillful, I mean it blends naturally into the story that already exists. It surprises the audience with new developments, yet still feels so reasonable that you accept it immediately. Nothing feels forced; if anything, it feels like this was the inevitable direction all along.

To me, Jujutsu Kaisen leans more toward a mature, seinen-like style. Compared with a near-contemporary title like Demon Slayer, this work presents a world that is clearly more complex and darker. In other words, justice does not automatically exist or prevail over evil; malicious people are not easily defeated, and sometimes they even win. These are defining traits of this series.

Against that backdrop, by the time Higuruma appears, protagonist Itadori Yuji (虎杖 悠仁, Itadori Yūji) is already carrying intense guilt from the Shibuya Incident that should never have belonged to him in the first place. He moves like the walking dead, filled with self-blame for his own helplessness and guilt toward his companions. He is nearly at his breaking point, uncertain about the boundary between justice and evil, and even starting to question whether he can keep holding on to a sense of justice at all.

## Higuruma Hiromi's Entrance

Higuruma has a similar background. The justice he insists on clearly has merit. Yet the dilemma he faces also reflects, with striking realism, the difficulty that many legal professionals must face: because of the complexity of law, the justice we uphold is not always understood by society. Even if you enter the courtroom with ideals, you may still be forced to accept rulings that contradict your values, even from people who appear equally trained in law.

So for me, if we talk about judicial reform, it may involve two layers at once. One is addressing legal technicians who hold flawed values despite professional expertise. The other is bridging the gap in understanding between ordinary people and legal professionals.

In the end, Higuruma also runs into this dilemma. But unlike ordinary people who can only accept a judgment, he receives a new tool right as he mentally collapses, allowing him to enforce his own version of justice through cursed technique.

## The Two Sides of Justice

Let's start with an interesting question: Higuruma kills judges and prosecutors who have chosen to blind themselves to reality. Is that an act of justice?

Justice should not be reduced to a cheap emotional high. If we stand in Higuruma's position, his actions do carry one side of justice. But as obvious as it may be, killing for justice also clearly carries a side that betrays justice.

\
Higuruma himself, of course, understands this, and that is reflected in his dialogue with Itadori.

At first, he says: "Killing someone you hate feels even better than you imagine." That line reveals one side of his behavior: self-justification.

But after his fight with Itadori ends, he asks essentially the same question again. This time, as if receiving salvation, he voices what is in his heart - perhaps the side he has longed to admit most: "Killing someone by your own will feels f***ing awful, doesn't it?"

\
This is the duality of our actions, and to me, it is also the pain and struggle inside Higuruma.

To close your eyes, or to keep them open wide - which one truly brings us closer to justice?

## Mutual Salvation

![](../Shared/death_penalty.png "Source: https://tempenensis.tumblr.com/post/668475812811554816/chapter-166")

\
On another level, we also know this: in a world where maybe everyone wants to be a good person, for reasons we do not fully understand, we often end up becoming the villain in someone else's story.

There are never simple answers in this world. We move forward on roads full of thorns, trying to find a path that belongs to us, even if the process leaves us battered.

And in moments of confusion and struggle, we may yearn for salvation - to be understood, accepted, forgiven, and loved.

\
In this sequence, Itadori's burden is bloodstained hands and a guilt he cannot erase. Higuruma's burden is the contradiction and struggle within the very goals of his actions, along with equally intense guilt.

Yet through Higuruma's ability, he almost by chance finds his own lost self within Itadori's actions. And in the end, Itadori receives a verdict of innocence from Higuruma. Each finds salvation in the other - and that, to me, is what makes this sequence so brilliant.

\
I also have to specifically praise the anime production quality here. The way Itadori's pure eyes and soul shine into Higuruma's already decayed heart and lead him back to his original convictions - even though I already knew what would happen from the manga, it was still unbelievably powerful. I was almost in tears.


# Wrapping Vulnerability in Anger

Justice is not the only thing with two sides. To me, human emotions are dual as well, especially anger.

Do you remember the last time you got angry? What were you angry about, and how did it end?

\
We are fortunate to live in a time when psychology and emotions are gradually being taken more seriously and better understood. There is also abundant psychological knowledge available to learn from.

One of the biggest things I have gained in recent years is understanding how a person can show strength while also revealing vulnerability.

\
After Itadori confesses that he killed people in Shibuya, Higuruma's first reaction is shock, followed by intense anger and doubt.

It is as if Itadori's behavior openly reveals vulnerability, forcing Higuruma to confront his own vulnerability as well, which then triggers his strong emotional response.

\
We are often the same. When someone displays intense anger, it usually also means there are intense vulnerable emotions underneath - maybe pain, disappointment, fear, or even helplessness.

For Higuruma, perhaps all of these vulnerable emotions coexist. By arming himself with anger, he tries to cover them up, making himself appear stronger and more powerful.

This may be a form of self-protection, but in doing so, we may also unintentionally hurt people we deeply care about - and perhaps hurt ourselves as well.

\
Emotion is a double-edged blade. Intense anger is a powerful outward release, yet what it often leaves behind is an inner emptiness after everything has been drained.

I feel fortunate to witness Higuruma's final choice: to face his own heart, share his vulnerability with Itadori, and find salvation through that.
