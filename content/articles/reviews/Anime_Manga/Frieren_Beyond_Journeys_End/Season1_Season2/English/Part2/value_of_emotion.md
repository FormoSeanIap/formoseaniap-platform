I think the second major theme in Season 1 is emotional communication—or, put differently, the importance of facing emotion.

Compared to the clearly structured setup of "the weight of time" in the beginning, the importance of emotional communication is spread throughout the entire first season.

For example, Frieren's feelings toward Himmel, or Fern's feelings toward Heiter.

Human emotion is complex. We have countless layered feelings, and we may spend our whole lives learning how to read and express them.

When Stark (シュタルク) first appears [1], what we are most directly shown is fear of oneself. Other than anger, this may be one of the most immediate, bone-deep emotions—because fear is both our survival instinct and one of the hardest emotions to face.

## Recognizing Emotion

For feelings like these, the first answer the story gives is: "acknowledge that they exist."

Just as Eisen (アイゼン) openly admits he is afraid, we do not need to be ashamed of admitting our emotions. In fact, those very emotions are what allow us to grow into who we are now.

This is not easy—especially in East Asian societies that often normalize emotional blackmail and the suppression of one's own feelings. Having the courage to acknowledge, and even express, your emotions is truly difficult.

That may also be why we so often see emotionally expressive characters in Japanese dramas, or characters who do seemingly irrational things in order to express emotion. Perhaps that reflects both the suppression of emotion in Japanese society and the longing to release it.

## Facing Emotion

At the same time, Stark's actions show us the next theme: beyond acknowledging emotion, we also need the courage to face it directly.

Humans are special in this way: not only can we feel emotions like other living beings, but we can also step back and observe and analyze our own feelings from a meta perspective. More than that, we can try to face emotions with reason, and even act in ways that seem to go against what we feel.

Stark is terrified when facing the dragon. He even blurts out "you old hag" when he mistakenly thinks Frieren is not coming to save him. Yet feeling his own resolve and grateful for the villagers' kindness, he still confronts the dragon despite his fear of death, and even acts against his own immediate emotions to protect the village.


But this is also exactly what makes humans special—or complex. Not only can we use reason to do things that seem to go against feeling, we can also go the other way and use feeling to do things that seem to go against reason.

Take Sein's (ザイン, Sein) arc [2]. After years of wrestling with regret, he knows rationally that his childhood friend's chance of being alive on that journey is very low, and the chance of finding him is even lower.

Yet in the end, after his brother gives him a push, he avoids the path of "if only I had done it back then" regret.

Still, choosing to face one's emotions honestly may itself be the result of a rational choice.

## Communicating Emotion

Going one step further, the work also reminds us that beyond facing our own emotions, a third thing is equally important: expressing our feelings.

Traces of this are everywhere in the story—from Frieren's feelings toward Himmel onward. The scene that impressed me the most, however, comes after the First-Class Mage Exam arc [3]: Serie's (ゼーリエ) disciple Lernen (レルネン) is even willing to try to kill Frieren so that his master will remember him [4].

When I first watched that part, my first reaction was: what on earth is this absurd behavior? But immediately after came an inexplicable sadness.

What this scene wants to convey is simple. Yet the greatest truths are simple: the courage to communicate feeling is pure, but also so difficult that people struggle to speak it aloud.
