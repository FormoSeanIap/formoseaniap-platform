As mentioned earlier, what is so fascinating about this work is the ambiguity of its core theme.

The story takes place in a world where the Demon King has already been defeated by the hero. In this era of peace, there is no clear grand objective, and the characters do not truly hold some great driving ambition. They simply move through ordinary daily life and, in that process, recall all kinds of small moments from the past.

I keep wondering: why can this kind of work create such a huge wave of popularity in today's society? How is it different from the stories we used to know—stories with clear goals and visible character growth?

## Works of the Past

In classic mainstream shonen-style works of the past, the protagonist usually establishes a clear goal, grows through many events, and eventually achieves that goal.

Typical examples include *Naruto* (-ナルト-), *One Piece* (ワンピース), *Bleach* (BLEACH), and *Hunter × Hunter* (ハンター×ハンター). Even in more recent works, this pattern remains common—for example, *Demon Slayer* (鬼滅の刃) and *Jujutsu Kaisen* (呪術廻戦).

The strength of this narrative design is clear direction, visible growth trajectories, and a strong sense of achievement for the audience. Even works that are not morally black-and-white—such as *Death Note* (デスノート) or *Berserk* (ベルセルク)—still tend to center on clear goals and character development.

But as similar works kept evolving and innovating, more stories began trying to break this framework.

For example, *Attack on Titan* (進撃の巨人) brings out a stronger sense of reality where "effort does not always guarantee success." Meanwhile, *Chainsaw Man* (チェンソーマン) carries an even stronger feeling that "the goal itself can be somewhat arbitrary." On top of that, many isekai-reincarnation stories feature protagonists who start with overwhelming power and therefore need little growth.

## Works Reflect Society

I believe works that become popular in a given era often reflect certain traits or needs of that society.

We generally expect that with effort, we can eventually reach the goals we set. That is why classic goal-driven stories remain timeless.

But perhaps when society still has room for growth, stories where effort leads to success are not only narratively satisfying, but also something people can occasionally witness in real life. In that context, such narratives naturally match audience expectations.

\
Then, at a certain point—when social growth gradually saturates and slows—the reality that effort may not produce clear outcomes becomes more widespread. Stories that preach "effort leads to clear results" can begin to sound grating, almost like lectures from older generations.

So in contrast, stories that say "it's okay not to get a good result," or stories where protagonists gain overpowered abilities instantly in another world, or stories where effort still does not guarantee success, can feel strangely healing when we ourselves feel powerless in real life.

And as the times continue to change, we not only stop obsessively chasing clear goals and progress—we turn and look more closely at our seemingly unremarkable lives, and by carefully savoring the everyday, we regain the ability to heal ourselves.

That, to me, is why works like *Frieren: Beyond Journey's End* can resonate so strongly in contemporary society.

## Trivial Daily Life

From the very beginning, this work keeps emphasizing all kinds of tiny everyday details.

For instance, the story almost never narrates in detail how the hero defeated the Demon King—as if those legendary feats matter far less than the mundane little moments during the journey.

\
In the First-Class Mage Exam arc [1], Wirbel (ヴィアベル), captain of the northern mage unit, talks about his impression of hero Himmel.
As a child, he was certainly captivated by the great legends: conquering complex labyrinths, defeating dragons, and fighting powerful demons.

But as he says, those grand legends have little to do with people's daily lives. Most people already exhaust themselves simply trying to protect their own ordinary routines.

So what villagers truly remember are small, trivial things. And strangely, those trivial things are precious; when the villagers share those seemingly insignificant moments, that is when they feel joy from the heart.

## An Unremarkable Life

Maybe that is exactly true.

As social development moves toward a kind of saturation, stories of riding social momentum and achieving success through effort alone increasingly feel less realistic.

The same level of effort today can no longer create outcomes as clearly visible as in previous decades. Stories of the so-called "successful life" lose persuasive power, and a shift in life goals starts to feel natural.

\
We begin to admit that our lives are just so-so, half-pushed and half-compromised. No great achievements—just getting by.

But even a modest life, even boring daily trivialities, can still be meaningful, still worth cherishing, and still able to make us genuinely happy.

From the broader trajectory of social development, this seems perfectly understandable. We may still have goals, but we can also enjoy the process; and if we can enjoy the process, then perhaps the result is not quite as important.

Still, truly internalizing this view of life and practicing it in daily living—being fully aware of the meaning in carefully feeling each ordinary day—is not simple at all.

\
Perhaps we all need that spell that can create a garden.

## Getting to Know a Person Again

For me, the part where Wirbel describes his impression of Himmel is also deeply insightful.

How do we usually get to know a person?

I think at first we almost always know people through labels: their profession, achievements, appearance, and so on.

\
Himmel's biggest label might be "the hero who defeated the Demon King," perhaps along with many different legends and stories.

But in today's rapidly changing society, perhaps we have fewer chances than before to truly know someone.

To the villagers, more than "the hero who defeated the Demon King," the person they truly knew was simply someone who helped repair their houses.

And maybe that is the real way to know a person: through everyday interactions, through seemingly trivial moments, to know the real them—not just the labels attached to them.

\
Yes, labels may be where knowing someone starts. But if we stay only at the surface of labels, we can never truly know that person.

I also hope people know me not through labels, but as a real person—not merely through certain outcomes, but through the process of how I became who I am.

I think this is especially important in contemporary society.
