At the time of writing this, Season 2 of *Frieren: Beyond Journey's End* has already been airing for a while.
This work has many seemingly strange qualities: a plot so calm it can feel almost uneventful, and a protagonist with no very explicit goal—yet it has still sparked huge popularity in today's world and become one of the most beloved anime.

That made me want to look deeper into why. Why can a seemingly quiet story resonate so strongly?
I tried to analyze this question from my own perspective, combined with my observations of society, and hoped to find some interesting insights.

That is probably why this article was born.

\
There are spoilers for at least Season 1 and the first half of Season 2. Please read at your own discretion.

## The Weight of Time

As for the first major theme introduced in Season 1, what I felt first was the weight of time.

Imagine you have a pet whose lifespan may only be around ten years. You form a deep bond with them, but through everyday life, you slowly forget that bond's fragility and get used to their presence.

Because your lifespan far exceeds your pet's, you unconsciously assume they will always be by your side as usual—until one day, you suddenly realize they have grown old, and then, in what feels like an instant, they are gone forever.

Only then do you realize that because your lifespans are different, you and your pet place time at different points on the balance. To you, their presence had become routine; to them, that same time was immeasurably precious.

So even though you still have a long, long life ahead, in the time that remains, you can only repeatedly revisit your memories with them. Those memories become your only treasure, because you know they are now the only connection between you and them.

## A Fifty-Year Meteor Shower

The opening "fifty-year meteor shower" immediately brings in this feeling of time's weight. From Frieren's (フリーレン) casual view of that length of time [1], to the moment she finally realizes that Himmel (ヒンメル) has already left her [1], she truly comes to understand that weight.

Compared to Frieren, Fern (フェルン), though often expressionless, may have been fully aware of Heiter's (ハイタ) finite life from the beginning—perhaps because she lost her parents young. That is why, when Heiter fell ill, she forced herself to keep training [2].

As she tells Frieren, her progress cannot be "someday." Because the only thing that is guaranteed to happen "someday" is death [2].

This opening already reveals the style of the work: the weight of time, the meaning of life, fear of death, the emotion of regret, the courage to express feelings, and more—these are all themes the story wants to offer its audience.

## Saying Goodbye to Others, Day by Day

I think this sense of time's weight has also been deeply illuminating for me.

Beyond pets, the clearest examples for me are noticing my parents grow older, and seeing changes in close friends around me.

In ordinary daily life, we gradually get used to the people around us, and in that process we forget life's impermanence. Then one day, we suddenly realize they have grown old—or even left us—and only then are we shocked by the weight of time.

Deep down, we all understand this. We just sometimes need works like this to remind us.

## Growing Distant from Ourselves, Day by Day

But beyond our relationships with others, I think we can also feel the weight of time through our relationship with ourselves.

The feeling of watching someone else's child grow from birth is subtle; and seeing how others' way of addressing you shifts from "older brother/sister" to "uncle/aunt" is even more complicated—because it feels like witnessing the death of your own youth.

If yesterday's me was a real existence, that self has already turned away and is moving farther with every tomorrow.

\
Yes, every day I am also saying goodbye to my past self, even though in my daily life with myself I, too, gradually forget and get used to my own existence.

I forget that existence itself is a miracle. I forget that existence itself can carry meaning. And perhaps only on the day I grow old will I suddenly realize I can no longer find those past selves that have long drifted away from me.

Then I realize that every day I exist now is the youngest day of my life in this world—the day on which I still have the most vitality and possibility to create meaning.

If that is true, what reason do I have not to cherish this day? What reason do I have not to love this still-young self?
