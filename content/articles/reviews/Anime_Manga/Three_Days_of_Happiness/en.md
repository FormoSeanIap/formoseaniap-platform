If there really were a set of scales that could measure the value of a life, what kind of weight could possibly balance against the little time we have left?

*Three Days of Happiness* tries to answer this ancient question through a setup that is both cruel and gentle at the same time.

It’s a story that makes you cry as you read it, yet leaves a small, warm light in your heart.

After all, the value of a life can never be measured by a single scale.

(Spoilers below)

## Measuring with Money and Social Recognition

The story begins by offering what seems like the simplest way to measure life: converting it into money. Money comes from work, and work represents social recognition. In this way, the value of a life is pressed into a cold receipt, as if merely functioning effectively within the machinery of society is enough to give a person a “price.”

The protagonist trades thirty years of his remaining life for three hundred thousand yen, and in doing so, he is left with only three months to live.

But is that really all a life is worth?

## Gaining the Weight of Existence Through Others’ Understanding

The first major turning point comes when the protagonist reunites with his childhood friend. In that moment, the scales tilt in another direction: the value of a life can also be granted by those who truly understand us.

In other words, even if we are not understood by society at large, as long as a few important people truly see and understand us, our lives gain meaning and weight. Yet, this kind of value still rests on how others see us. In other words, it is still an external measure.

As the story shows, when the protagonist ultimately loses all of these important people forever, the value of his life collapses along with them.

My own interpretation is this: the most impossible thing in this world is, after all, genuine understanding between people.

## The Value of Self-Recognition

And yet, it is precisely at this point that the story quietly replaces the weight on the scales with a third kind of measure: self-recognition.

We keep searching outward for the meaning of life, while ignoring the fact that the most important answer has always been within ourselves.

The heroine, who works as an observer invisible to society, begins to feel drawn to the protagonist when, for the first time, he chooses to live in order to face his own heart, even going so far as to talk to her in public, looking like a madman speaking to thin air, without caring about how others see him.

At that moment, the value of his life is clearly affirmed in a completely different way. On the one hand, his remaining lifespan was originally worth only thirty thousand yen; on the other hand, in the heroine’s eyes, his life is worth thirty million, or even three billion.

This is one of the most delicate aspects of the work: it reminds us that value does not need to be bestowed by others. We can define it ourselves.

## Ways of Defining Life’s Meaning

When his life is down to less than three months, the protagonist instead discovers a reason to live.

He stops trying to meet society’s expectations and begins to live in a way that is simple and purely for himself.

He talks with the heroine in front of everyone, fully aware that in their eyes he looks like a weirdo chatting with the air, yet he doesn’t care. Because happiness is flowing in that very moment, and for the first time, he truly has a life that “belongs to him.”

Ironically, society gradually begins to accept his strange behavior. More and more people start to believe in the heroine’s existence, and a magical yet strangely real atmosphere spreads through the story.

Why would such absurd behavior gain social recognition? As the work itself suggests, it’s probably because the protagonist is genuinely “happy from the bottom of his heart, and truly living for himself.” Happiness cannot be faked. A person who sincerely lives for themselves radiates a light that is hard to deny.

This, to me, is the most ingenious part of the work: through a simple premise, it manages to draw out multiple layers of meaning.

## Redefining “Being Rich”

In the end, the protagonist decides to sell off even more of his remaining life in exchange for money to help the heroine. Even though he has only one month left to sell, his life, now filled with meaning, rises to an unprecedented value.

And so, when both of them are left with only three days to live, they smile at each other and say:

“Now we’re rich, aren’t we?”

The meaning of “rich” here is no longer just a number in money, but the fact that they have found a sense of life’s meaning that belongs only to the two of them.

After making peace with themselves, they finally manage to make peace with the world as well.

## Conclusion

If *Three Days of Happiness* urges us to think about the value of life, then what it truly does is gently place the scales back into our own hands.

How much a life is worth is never for the world to decide. It depends on who we are willing to live for, and what we are willing to live for.
